**Nonhuman** is a self-descriptive term for those who identify at least partially as anything other than human, in any way. It is adjacent to the broader [alterhuman] umbrella in that not all nonhumans are alterhuman (as alterhuman is an opt-in term), and not all alterhumans are nonhuman (alterhumanity includes many who identify only as human). Whether any given nonhuman also identifies as [otherkin] is a matter of personal preference; some do, and some don't.

Unlike many other identity definitions, nonhuman is an intentionally broad and vague term with minimal requirements for its use. Anybody who is in any way, shape, part, sense, or form other-than-human may opt into referring to themself as nonhuman. Nonhumans might or might not identify as human as well as nonhuman. Many are attracted to the label for its lack of established gatekeepers.

## Misconceptions and misusage

- Nonhumans do not necessarily believe themselves to be physically nonhuman. Many do, and many do not. The term nonhuman shouldn't automatically be assumed to mean physically nonhuman.
- Nonhumans are not necessarily animals. Anything that is other-than-human may be the basis for a nonhuman identity, including objects, plants, and concepts.
- There is no single correct way to be nonhuman. Spiritual nonhumans, psychological nonhumans, physical nonhumans, voluntary nonhumans, and any other variant or origin are all equally nonhuman.
- Not all nonhumans are otherkin. Otherkin is a distinct subculture in which any given nonhuman may or may not be a member.

## History

Modern nonhuman subcultures trace their roots to the late 20th century.[^1]

Various groups based on a shared identity of being elves, such as the Elf Queen's Daughters and the Silver Elves, were formed in the early 1970s. The word "otherkin" was coined in the 1990s in a mailing list for people who identified as elves or other fantasy creatures.

Independently, the draconic (dragon-identifying) and therian (animal-identifying) subcultures arose in the 1990s on Usenet groups.

For years, the otherkin community rejected robotkin and largely branded them trolls, as the most common narrative of how to correctly be otherkin stated that one's soul was that of one's kintype, and robots were perceived as lifeless objects and therefore incapable of having souls.[^2] As a result, many robotkin and other machine-identified people, having been rejected from the community, did not closely identify with the otherkin subculture and formed their own community. As time passed, the otherkin community became much more open-minded towards previously rejected identities, and robotkin is now seen as an accepted kintype. Still, not all machine-identified people label themselves as otherkin for various reasons and may prefer to use the label nonhuman.
